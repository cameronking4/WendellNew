Research

My work productively situates fundamental questions of twentieth-century U.S. history—civil rights, racial identity, radicalism, surveillance, and state power—within a North American frame. It examines the cross-border activism of persecuted persons in the United States and Canada, tracing the people who led and the ideas that shaped racial struggles in twentieth-century North America.

My current project, “From the North Star to the Black Star: African North Americans and the Search for a Homeland in Canada, 1919 to 1985,” integrates and juxtaposes freedom struggles in Canada and the United States. It illuminates the internationalism of white supremacy, exploring how borderlands peoples imagined freedom and tried to subvert the hegemony of the nation state. I reveal how the Cold War and sweeping domestic shifts enabled a restructuring of civil rights in the United States, on one hand, and a human rights regime in Canada, on the other. Using declassified sources, I expose the only known case of collusion and counter-subversion between the U.S. and Canadian security and intelligence apparatuses to undermine black citizenship on both sides of the border and in the Anglophone Caribbean in the 1960s and 1970s.

I have begun research on the histories of deportation in North America and African American (self-)exile.

Teaching

  • I believe that the most important sensibility for a teacher is empathy—our capacity to humanize the “other,” especially those whom people or institutions render invisible, exploitable, or expendable. This ethos undergirds my approach to studying and teaching the history of African-descended peoples in the Americas.
  • I have taught as a Lead Instructor in the Yale Young Global Scholars initiative, offering in-depth seminars as diverse as Bacon’s Rebellion and the Making of U.S. Racial Caste; John Locke and the Imperial Foundations of Property Rights; The Haitian Revolution; The Monroe Doctrine; Financial Missionaries and Dollar Diplomacy; The U.S. Empire; Comparative Apartheid in South Africa and the United States; Decolonization in Africa; and Civil and Voting Rights Acts of 1964 and 1965.
  • I have served as a Teaching Fellow for The Civil War and Reconstruction Era lecture, facilitating two reading discussions and providing detailed feedback on student writing and performance. Since 2014, I have guest lectured for the Yale College course Quebec and Canada, 1791 to Present, for which I am a secondary reader on select written assignments.

    Service

    As an undergraduate, I worked in community development, and social and public policy at the municipal, provincial, and federal levels. Upon graduation, I conducted research for several think-tanks, and served as a case manager, from 2009 to 2012, on a multi-million dollar, federally-funded, University of Toronto-evaluated youth gang prevention and intervention project in north Toronto. I champion access to quality education for impoverished students, and promote peace-building and inter-ethnic dialogue in sub-Saharan Africa. My advocacy in behalf of poor, marginalized communities has earned me a YMCA Peace Medallion, an Ontario Medal for Young Volunteers, a Toronto B.M.E. Police and Community Award of Excellence, among others.

    I am a board member of the Inspirit Foundation, a national organization that supports young change agents and promotes pluralism and citizenship in Canada through impact investing and the arts. I serve also on the board of the Tujenge Africa Foundation, a registered 501(c)(3) that identifies, trains, and incubates some of the brightest young women and men for post-secondary education and civic leadership in Central and Eastern Africa. The Foundation is a 2015 recipient of the highly-coveted Echoing Green Global Fellowship.
